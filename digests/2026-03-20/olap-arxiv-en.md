## 3. Why It Matters for OLAP

This paper goes directly at the architecture of analytical query engines. OLAP systems have historically traded flexibility for performance, relying on complex vectorized execution engines, cost-based optimizers, code generation, and storage/runtime abstractions that inevitably introduce overheads. GenDB suggests a more radical path: synthesize the execution artifact itself, potentially bypassing much of the machinery of conventional engines when serving analytical workloads.

If this approach proves robust, it could reshape how we think about query optimization, engine extensibility, and benchmarking. It is highly relevant to embedded engines like DuckDB, cloud warehouses, and specialized OLAP systems because it raises the possibility of per-query or per-workload engine generation rather than static system design. Even if the current prototype has limitations, the idea is important: future OLAP performance may come less from manually engineered operators and more from automated synthesis loops guided by workload and hardware feedback.
