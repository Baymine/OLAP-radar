- **Research Problem:** General-purpose OLAP engines pay unavoidable overheads from abstraction, runtime schema interpretation, and broad workload support, even when actual workloads are stable and narrow. Building workload-specific engines by hand is too expensive and slow.

## 3. Why It Matters for OLAP

For OLAP practitioners, this paper is notable because it reframes optimization beyond classic cost-based planning, indexing, and vectorized execution. Instead of asking how to run a workload well on an existing engine, it asks whether the engine itself should be synthesized around the workload. That has major implications for recurring BI dashboards, embedded analytics, benchmark-specialized systems, and tenant-specific serving layers where query patterns are predictable.

It also connects to several active themes in modern data infrastructure: code generation, adaptive physical design, specialization for hardware, and the tradeoff between maintainability and peak performance. In lakehouse or warehouse settings, a synthesized engine could become a new serving tier for high-value workloads while the general engine remains the system of record. For researchers, it raises important benchmarking and validation questions: how to fairly compare bespoke synthesized systems against mature OLAP engines, how to measure synthesis cost, and how robust such systems remain as workloads drift.
