- **Publication Date:** 2026-03-02  
- **Research Problem:** Modern OLAP engines pay unavoidable overhead for supporting arbitrary schemas, operators, and workloads, even when production deployments often serve relatively stable analytical patterns. Building a specialized engine manually is too costly and complex to be practical.

## 3. Why It Matters for OLAP

This work is highly relevant to **query engine architecture** because it questions whether the next generation of OLAP systems should be monolithic engines at all, versus synthesized, workload-specialized runtimes. It also matters for **optimization and storage layout**, since a bespoke engine can remove abstraction overhead, hardwire schema knowledge, and choose data structures and execution strategies aligned with a known query set. For **lakehouse and warehouse operators**, the idea is especially compelling in environments with repeated dashboard, BI, and benchmark-like workloads where specialization could translate into large cost and latency reductions. More broadly, it may reshape how the community thinks about **benchmarking**: instead of comparing fixed engines only, we may need to compare synthesis systems that generate engines for given analytical workloads.
